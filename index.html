Neuroplasticity

Week 7 · Neuroplasticity

What Is Neuroplasticity?

🧠 The Simple Idea

The brain's ability to change and adapt throughout life. Not fixed — it rewires itself based on experience.

⚡ When It Happens

  • Learning new skills
  • Recovering from injury
  • Ageing
  • Disease processes

🔗 Key Mechanism

Synaptogenesis — new connections form between neurons. "Neurons that fire together, wire together!"

🏥

Nursing Example: A stroke patient re-learning to walk is neuroplasticity in action. The brain reroutes signals around the damaged area through therapy and repetition.

Spielman, Jenkins & Lovett (2020). Psychology 2e. OpenStax.

Week 7 · Neuroplasticity

Building & Trimming Connections

🌱 Synaptogenesis

The brain grows new synapses. Happens when we learn, practise, or experience new things.


Every clinical skill you practise builds new neural pathways!

✂️ Synaptic Pruning

The brain removes weak or unused connections to work more efficiently — like clearing a cluttered filing cabinet.


Peaks in teenage years. Continues throughout life.

💡

Real-Life Evidence: London taxi drivers develop denser grey matter in the hippocampus from memorising thousands of routes. Imagine what nursing training does to YOUR brain! (Maguire et al., 2000)

Spielman, Jenkins & Lovett (2020). Psychology 2e. OpenStax.

Week 7 · Plasticity in Practice

Plasticity in Rehabilitation

🤖 Robotic Therapy

Robots assist stroke patients to move limbs, triggering plasticity to regain motor function. (Amirabdollahian et al., 2003)

🦾 Prosthetic Limbs

Extended use reorganises the brain — motor areas begin functioning like those of a typical person. (van den Heiligenberg et al., 2018)

🥽 Virtual Reality

Chronic pain patients using 360° VR therapy reported a 60% reduction in symptoms. (Jones, Moore & Choo, 2016)

🗣️ Multilingualism

Speaking multiple languages increases grey & white matter density. The brain literally grows! (Mechelli et al., 2004)

👩‍⚕️

Why it matters: Understanding plasticity helps nurses support patient rehab, explain recovery timelines, and advocate for evidence-based therapies that harness the brain's ability to heal.

Quiz Time! 🎯 · Neuroplasticity

Check Your Understanding

1. Which process describes the brain creating new connections between neurons?

B. Synaptogenesis is correct. The brain forms new synapses when we learn something new — the foundation of neuroplasticity and why rehabilitation works.

2. True or False: The adult brain stops changing after the age of 25.

FALSE. The brain retains plasticity throughout life. Synaptogenesis and pruning continue into old age, though they slow down. This is why lifelong learning — and nursing CPD — works!

Week 8 · Personality & Performance

Personality & Performance

🧬 Trait Theory

Personality is innate, stable and enduring. You are born with it and it stays consistent across situations.


🩺 A naturally calm nurse handles emergencies with composure.

👀 Social Learning Theory

Behaviour is learned by observing and copying role models. Shaped by environment — not fixed.


🩺 A student adopts good bedside manner by watching senior nurses.

🤫 Introverts

Prefer low arousal. Thrive in tasks needing concentration & precision.

🩺 Surgical nursing, medication dispensing, detailed care planning.

🎉 Extroverts

Need high arousal. Excel in fast-paced, social environments.

🩺 A&E, community nursing, patient education.

Trait Theory; Bandura's Social Learning Theory

Week 8 · Motivation

What Drives Us?

❤️ Intrinsic Motivation

Comes from within. Pride, satisfaction, love of the job. Long-lasting and powerful.


🩺 Feeling fulfilled after supporting a patient through a difficult diagnosis.

🏆 Extrinsic Motivation

Comes from outside. More temporary.
Tangible: pay, certificates.
Intangible: praise, recognition.


🩺 A "thank you" card from a patient's family.

⚠️ Key Balance

Overusing extrinsic rewards can undermine intrinsic motivation. Intangible rewards (praise, recognition) are generally more effective long-term than tangible ones.

Heider (1958) Attribution Theory; McClelland et al. (1953)

Week 8 · Achievement Motivation

NACH vs NAF: Two Types of Mindset

🚀 NACH — Need to Achieve

  • Seeks challenges
  • Not afraid of failure
  • Persists under pressure
  • Values feedback
  • Attributes success to effort

🩺 Volunteers for complex cases to grow their skills.

😟 NAF — Need to Avoid Failure

  • Avoids difficult tasks
  • Fears being evaluated
  • Attributes success to luck
  • Performs worse when watched

🩺 Avoids clinical placements out of fear of mistakes.

💬

Reflection: How might you support a NAF colleague? Mentoring, constructive feedback and a safe learning environment can shift someone towards a NACH mindset. (Atkinson, 1957)

Week 8 · Aggression

Understanding Aggression

📖 Definition

"Any behaviour directed towards harming another who is motivated to avoid such treatment." (Baron, 1977)


Requires intent — accidents are NOT aggression.

⚡ Direct vs Indirect

Direct: Shouting at a colleague or patient.

Indirect: Slamming equipment out of frustration.

🧬 Trait/Instinct Theory

Aggression is genetically inherited — an innate instinct. Competition brings it to the surface.

👁️ Social Learning Theory

Aggression is learned by watching others. If rewarded or unchallenged, it is repeated. (Bandura's Bobo Doll)

🏥

Nursing Context: Healthcare workers face aggression from patients or relatives. Understanding the causes — frustration, fear, pain — helps nurses de-escalate with empathy. (Endersen & Olweus, 2005)

Week 8 · Arousal & Performance

Arousal Theories: From Drive to the Zone

📈 Drive Theory (Hull, 1943)

More arousal = better performance. A straight line up. Works best for well-learned skills only.


⚠️ Problem: Even experts "choke" — Drive Theory can't explain this.

🔔 Inverted-U Theory (Yerkes & Dodson, 1908)

There is an optimal arousal point. Too little or too much = worse performance. Forms an upside-down U shape.

😴

Too Little Arousal

Bored. Unfocused. Miss details.

🩺 Nurse on quiet night shift misses patient deterioration.

🎯

IZOF — The Zone

Relaxed, focused, in control. Peak performance.

🩺 Nurse calm and sharp during a cardiac arrest response.

😰

Too Much Arousal

Overwhelmed. Errors increase.

🩺 Student nurse so anxious in OSCE they forget basic steps.

Drive Theory (Hull, 1943); Inverted-U (Yerkes & Dodson, 1908); IZOF (Hanin, 1980)

Week 8 · Psychological Strategies to Enhance Performance

SMART Goals, SCAT & STAI

SMART Goal Setting — Nursing Example
SSpecific"I will improve my cannulation technique."
MMeasurable"I will practise 3x per week and track my first-attempt success rate."
AAchievable"My mentor has agreed to supervise and give feedback each session."
RRealistic"Achievable within my current placement and skill level."
TTime-bound"80% first-attempt success within 6 weeks."

📋 SCAT

Sport Competition Anxiety Test — questionnaire measuring how anxious someone typically gets in competitive situations. Linked to personality. (Martens, 1977)

📊 STAI

State-Trait Anxiety Inventory — measures trait anxiety (your general tendency) AND state anxiety (how you feel right now). Widely used in healthcare. (Spielberger, 1983)

Martens (1977); Spielberger (1983)

Quiz Time! 🎯 · Multiple Choice

Test Your Knowledge

1. A patient shouts at a nurse because they're scared. According to Baron (1977), is this aggression?

C is correct. Baron (1977) defines aggression as behaviour intended to harm. A frightened patient shouting out of fear is likely not acting aggressively — context and intent matter enormously in nursing practice.

2. Which best describes the SMART goal "I will achieve 80% first-attempt cannulation success within 6 weeks"?

B is correct. The goal is specific (cannulation), measurable (80%), achievable (with practice and supervision), realistic (for a student nurse) and time-bound (6 weeks). All five SMART criteria are met!

3. Which theory proposes that performance always improves in a straight line as arousal increases?

C. Drive Theory (Hull, 1943) proposes a linear relationship — more arousal always means better performance. However this was criticised because even highly skilled people choke under extreme pressure, which Drive Theory cannot explain.
Quiz Time! ✅❌ · True or False Final Round

True or False — Final Round!

1. Extroverts perform best in quiet, low-stimulation environments.

FALSE. Extroverts need high arousal to perform at their best — they thrive in exciting, fast-paced settings like A&E. It is introverts who prefer low-stimulation environments requiring concentration and precision.

2. Intangible rewards (e.g. praise) are generally more effective for long-term motivation than tangible ones (e.g. money).

TRUE. Research consistently shows intangible rewards sustain intrinsic motivation better. Over-relying on tangible rewards can actually erode an individual's inner drive — crucial for nursing management and student support.

3. The SCAT measures both an individual's general anxiety AND their anxiety in the current moment.

FALSE. The SCAT measures typical responses to competitive situations (trait anxiety only). It is the STAI that measures BOTH general (trait) AND current (state) anxiety. Don't mix them up!
Summary · Weeks 7 & 8

Key Takeaways

🧠 Neuroplasticity

Brain rewires through experience. Rehab works because of plasticity. Keep learning!

🎭 Personality

Trait vs Social Learning. Know your type — intro or extrovert — and use it wisely.

🔥 Motivation

Intrinsic beats extrinsic long-term. NACH mindset = seek challenges, grow from failure.

👊 Aggression

Intent matters. De-escalate with empathy. Assertion ≠ aggression.

⚡ Arousal

Drive Theory → Inverted-U → IZOF. Find your zone. Not too little, not too much.

🎯 Strategies

SMART goals. Measure anxiety with SCAT & STAI. Plan, prepare, perform.
